  • Make a Quick List of What’s Late: Identify the most urgent tasks and focus on them first.
  • Set a Timer and Just Start: Even 15–30 minutes of focused work can help you regain momentum.
  • Let Go of Perfection: If you’re hesitating because you want your work to be “just right,” focus on finishing first. You can revise later.

  • Set Small, Daily Goals: Even if you’re on track now, pacing yourself will help prevent last-minute stress later.
  • Build in Buffer Time: Give yourself extra time to review and revise instead of rushing before the deadline.
  • Stay Engaged in Discussions: Active participation helps you keep up with key concepts, making assignments easier.

üìà How to Improve Group Discussions

Discussions are more useful when everyone contributes. Timeliness helps too. Here are some ideas that can help your group collaborate more smoothly.

  • Engage Early: Posting sooner rather than later gives your group more time to respond and interact.
  • Ask Questions in Your Replies: Instead of just agreeing, ask follow-up questions to keep the conversation going.
  • Summarize the Discussion: If your group’s conversation feels scattered, summarize key takeaways to bring clarity.
  • Check In with Your Group Members: If your group isn’t engaging, a quick message can encourage more participation. Find everyone’s contact information in the Introduce Yourself Discussion.

Documentation Options

Depending upon the system that you choose, you may use footnotes, endnotes, or in-text citations.

These three options differ in the information they include and in where they belong in the document. The exact details depend upon the documentation system you’re using. That said, here are the major differences to keep in mind:

Table 2. Documentation Options
Documentation Option Information Included Location of the Information
Footnotes The name(s) of the author(s), source title, and relevant page number or similar details. Footnote number immediately follows the relevant information in the document. Documentation information is at the bottom (the foot) of the page where the reference is mentioned.
Endnotes The name(s) of the author(s), source title, and relevant page number or similar details. Endnote number immediately after relevant information in the text of the document. Documentation information is on a separate page (or pages), at the end of a document, in a numbered list in the order they appear in the document.
In-Text Citations The name(s) of the author(s), sometimes the year of publication, and often the relevant page number or similar details. Typically in parentheses. Example: (Tyson, 2025). Either immediately after the reference in the document or at the end of the sentence (or other passage) that refers to the source.

Word Processor Documentation Tools

Most word processors have tools to streamline the process of adding documentation to your projects. Use the basic information below to find the tools and practice using them.

For more detailed information about these tools, check out the links to LinkedIn Learning videos and to documentation websites on the Documentations and Citations page in Canvas.

In Microsoft Word

Find documentation tools in the References tab on the ribbon. In the Footnotes section of the ribbon, use Insert Footnote or Insert Endnote to add the number reference to your document. Next, type your information in the footnote or endnote area.

Find tools for in-text citations and your bibliography in the Citations & Bibliography section of the ribbon. Choose Insert Citation to add a parenthetical citation. Use the Manage Sources, Style, and Bibliography commands to add your sources to the document and generate your reference list.

In Google Docs

Add footnotes by choosing the Insert menu and then the Footnotes command. You can then type your information in the footnote area.

Use the Tools menu and the Citations command to show the sidebar where you can add your sources. After adding your sources, you can add in-text citations by hovering over the source in the sidebar and using the Cite command.

Google Docs does not have an option for endnotes.
