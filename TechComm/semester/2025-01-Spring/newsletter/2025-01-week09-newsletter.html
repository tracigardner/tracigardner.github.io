üìù Advice for Strong Proposals

What separates a decent proposal from a strong, persuasive one? Clear, confident, and credible ideas!

Be Clear About the Problem You’re Solving

Readers need to understand why your project matters. What challenge or opportunity are you responding to? Avoid vague claims. Instead, ground your rationale in the scenario, user needs, or relevant research.

We chose the Personal Protective Equipment website because it impacts safety for on-campus workers, so it needs to be easy to understand and use. If workers can‘t find the details they need quickly, someone’s health may suffer.

Break Down Your Methods Thoughtfully

Your methods section should show you have a plan—but it doesn’t need to include every detail. Focus on the major steps, the tools or platforms you’ll use, and why those choices make sense for your audience and goals.

  • What types of research or analysis will you do?
  • How will you collect or organize information?
  • What formats or technologies will you use?

Write for a Reader Who Has Questions

Imagine a busy reader skimming your proposal: what would they need to know to say “yes” to your idea? Use headings, bullets, and concise language to guide their reading. Don’t make them search for your main points.

Final Tip: A good proposal doesn’t just explain your project—it convinces your reader that it’s worth doing and that your team is capable of doing it well.

üîó Writing Accessible, Clear Links

Avoid vague phrases like “click here” or just pasting in long URLs. Instead, write links that are:

  • Descriptive: Tell readers what they’ll find if they click.
  • Contextual: Fit the link naturally into your sentence.
  • Accessible: Use clear language for people using screen readers or keyboard navigation.

Whenever possible, use the actual title of the page you’re linking to. That helps readers recognize the content and confirms they’re in the right place when they click.

Good vs. Not-So-Good Examples

‚ùå Avoid:
Click here to see the assignment page.

Why it’s a problem: The phrase “Click here” doesn’t tell the reader what the link is for. It also assumes they’re using a mouse or trackpad.

Why it works: The link clearly describes what the page is about and fits smoothly into the sentence.

üíº Required Sections for Your Proposal

Each section of your proposal has a job to do—together, they show your readers what you're planning, why it matters, and how you’ll get it done.

Use this guide as you work through each part of the document with your group. For examples and explanations, check out the Proposal Assignment and the Annotated Proposal Template.

Table 1. Required Sections of Your Proposal
Section Description
Memo Headers
 Start with the basics. Use standard memo format with To, From, Date, and Subject fields. Be sure you use the correct name for the To and From, and include a Strong Subject Line.
Introduction This is your elevator pitch. Introduce the project you’re proposing, name your topic, and briefly state your goal. Think of it as the “why you’re writing” part.
Overview & Rationale Explain what your project will cover and why it matters. What problem are you addressing or what opportunity are you exploring? You’re making the case that your project matters—back it up with reasoning and maybe a little context.
Methods and Resources Here’s the how. Describe what you’ll do to complete the project and what tools or sources you’ll use. Be specific enough to sound prepared, but don’t overdo it.
Project Audience Tell us who your Recommendation Report is for and how you’re keeping their needs in mind. Focus on the person who can decide to implement the recommendations you suggest.
Personnel Who’s doing what? What are their qualifications? Outline each team member’s roles and responsibilities.
Timetable Break it down week by week or by project phases. Show you’ve thought through how and when everything will get done. Present the details with a Gantt Chart.
Conclusion Wrap it up by summarizing your proposal and emphasizing the value of your project. Keep it confident and forward-looking. Remember to include contact information so the reader can follow-up.
