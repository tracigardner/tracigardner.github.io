Introduction to Technical Writing (M 8/25–8/29)

This module is designed to introduce you to some of the most basic underlying principles of technical writing, which is a subset of the larger field of technical communication. Those principles are

Rhetoric

Rhetoric is the art of persuasive communication - how you use language, structure, and style to effectively convey your message and influence your audience.

In technical writing, rhetoric is crucial because you're not just dumping information - you're trying to help users accomplish specific goals.

Good technical writers use rhetorical principles to understand their audience, structure information strategically, choose the right tone and language, and anticipate and address potential concerns.

Ethics

Ethics is the study of what's right and wrong - the principles that guide moral decision-making about how we should act and treat others.

In technical writing, ethics matters because your work directly affects people's ability to use products safely and effectively.

The core ethical question for technical writers is: "Am I helping users succeed in a way that respects their safety, time, and dignity?" Sometimes this means pushing back when companies want to downplay problems or make unrealistic claims about their products. 

Audience

Audience refers to the specific people who will read your writing - their knowledge level, goals, context, and needs.

Audience awareness in technical writing means actively considering who your readers are and adapting your communication accordingly.

Good audience awareness leads to practical decisions like using familiar terminology, organizing information by priority, choosing appropriate detail levels, and anticipating common questions or pain points. 

 

Module Learning Outcomes

Once you complete this module, you should be able to

  • Demonstrate how rhetoric, ethics, and audience awareness work together in technical writing decisions
  • Critique existing technical documents using these three frameworks
  • Design user-centered documents that are rhetorically effective, ethically sound, and audience-appropriate
  • Justify writing choices by referencing principles of rhetoric, ethics, and audience awareness
