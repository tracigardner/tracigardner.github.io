Complexity to Clarity

Your non-expert readers are smart but not specialists. That means that your explanations must aim for clarity, not oversimplification.

Quick Moves

  • Define once, use consistently. Add a plain-language definition the first time a key term appears.
  • Swap jargon for everyday words. If a technical term is essential, pair it with a short explanation.
  • Use concrete examples. Tie abstract ideas to familiar contexts, numbers, or scenarios.
  • Chunk information. Short paragraphs, informative headings, and lists guide non-expert readers.
  • Prefer visuals with captions. Tables, simple charts, and labeled diagrams can carry heavy cognitive load.

Writing About Setbacks

Setbacks happen. In your progress report, use your professional tone to turn problems into plans. Here’s how:

  • State the issue briefly. Avoid blame or drama.
  • Explain the impact. What deadline, scope, or quality is affected?
  • Share what you tried. Show initiative and reasoning.
  • Propose a path forward. Specific actions, owners (if relevant), and dates.
  • Invite guidance. Ask 1–2 focused questions.

Three-Sentence Template

You can accomplish this with just three sentences. Tell your reader:

  1. What happened
  2. Why it matters
  3. What you’ll do next & when.

Here’s an example:

County-level outage data was unavailable. I contacted the utility and requested a weekly extract. I will incorporate the data when it arrives (expected by Oct 29).

Organizing Long Reports for Easy Reading

Structure is a reader’s first access to meaning. Make navigation effortless.

Core Structure

  • Front matter: Title, brief overview, and roadmap of sections.
  • Body sections: Group by purpose (methods, findings, implications), not by the order you researched.
  • Back matter: References and any appendices that would distract in the main flow.

Wayfinding Tactics

  • Descriptive headings: Replace “Results” with “What We Found About X.”
  • Logical sequencing: Put must-know information before nice-to-know details.
  • Parallel formatting: If one section uses bullets and a key takeaway line, all peer sections should too.
  • Signposts: Opening and closing sentences that tell readers what’s coming and what to do with it.

Micro-example: End sections with a one-line takeaway: “Bottom line: Option A reduces cost by ~12% with no change in reliability.”

The Real Purpose of a Progress Report

A progress report is not a victory lap; it is a planning tool for you and your supervisor.

What It Should Do

  • Show status honestly: What is complete, in progress, and not started.
  • Explain decisions: Why you prioritized certain tasks or sources.
  • Surface risks: What could block progress and what you are doing about it.
  • Request feedback: Targeted questions that help you proceed efficiently.
  • Map next steps: Specific actions with dates or checkpoints.

Useful Outline

  1. Purpose & scope (1–2 sentences)
  2. Completed work (specific, verifiable)
  3. Work in progress (what, why, when)
  4. Issues & risks (impact + mitigation)
  5. Next steps & timeline
  6. Questions for feedback

Micro-example: “Completed: Reviewed 12 peer-reviewed sources on rural broadband reliability; In progress: drafting Findings section; Risk: limited county-level outage data—requesting access from IT by Oct 29.”
