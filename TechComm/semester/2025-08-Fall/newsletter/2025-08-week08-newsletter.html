Use Strong Verbs

Readers will use your document to do something, so your verbs should help them act.

Use imperative verbs

  • Click the Start button.
  • Turn the knob clockwise until it clicks.
  • Save your file before closing the app.

Avoid passive or wordy phrasing

  • The Start button should be clicked by the user.
  • You will need to make sure the file is saved.

Why it matters: Imperative verbs shorten sentences, clarify the task, and give your reader confidence. In instructional writing, direct = helpful.

üî¢ Numbered vs. Bulleted Lists

Lists improve readability—but only if you choose the right kind.

Use numbered lists when order matters

  1. Plug in the device.
  2. Press the power button.
  3. Wait for the indicator light to turn green.

Use bulleted lists when order doesn’t matter

  • Phillips-head screwdriver
  • Two AA batteries
  • Replacement filter

Quick rule: If users must follow steps, number them. If they just need to check items, use bullets.

The right visuals clarify tasks; the wrong ones confuse.

Use images when they…

  • Show a process or part that’s hard to describe in words.
  • Reinforce—not replace—written instructions.
  • Are simple, cropped, and clearly labeled.

Always add clear captions

A caption tells readers what they’re seeing and why it matters.
