Word choice can subtly influence tone and credibility. Compare these pairs:

  • sprawl vs. growth
  • problem vs. challenge
  • claims vs. states
  • expensive vs. costly vs. high-cost

Even small shifts in connotation can make a report sound more objective. Choose terms that describe rather than judge. When in doubt, imagine your word being used in a government report—if it sounds too emotional for that context, revise it.

üß≠ Writing with Objectivity

Objectivity isn’t just about choosing neutral words—it’s about how you frame and balance information.

  • Represent all sides fairly: Include evidence or viewpoints from each major position on the issue. Avoid giving one side more space or emphasis unless the data clearly support it.
  • Use evidence, not opinion: Back every claim with data, examples, or expert sources. Replace phrases like “I believe” or “it seems” with verifiable facts.
  • Check your sources: Use credible, up-to-date, and balanced references. Be wary of advocacy groups that present only one perspective.
  • Be transparent about limits: If data are incomplete or uncertain, acknowledge it. Readers trust writers who recognize complexity.

Letter of Transmittal: What to Include

Your letter of transmittal introduces your report to its reader and establishes a professional tone. It should be brief—one page at most—and typically includes paragraphs on each of these topics:

  1. Introduction: Identify the report and state why you are submitting it (e.g., “I am pleased to submit my informational report on renewable packaging materials.”).
  2. Project Overview: Summarize the report’s main topic and what it covers.
  3. Key Findings: Note any key findings, trends, or points of interest.
  4. Conclusion & Contact Info: Thank the reader and offer to provide clarification or additional information.

See the Letter of Transmittal Example with Formatting Details for additional help.

Letter Format Reminders

Use standard block business letter format for your letter of transmittal:

  • Single-spaced, with double spaces between paragraphs
  • Left-aligned, no indentations
  • Your address (or company letterhead) at the top
  • Date below your address
  • Recipient’s name, title, organization, and address
  • A clear subject line (optional but helpful)
  • Formal salutation and closing (e.g., “Sincerely,”)
  • Include Enclosure Notation and list of the enclosures

Keep the tone polite and professional—this letter serves as the first impression of your report.
