“ ” Avoid Dropped Quotes

A dropped quote lacks introduction and explanation, leaving readers puzzled.

Let’s say you encounter this quotation in a document:

“The revolution began not with a bang, but with a whisper” (Smith 45).

After reading that, you’re likely to have some questions:

  • What do you mean by a bang?
  • Do you mean an explosion? a loud sound?
  • And a whisper? So it was quiet?
  • What the heck are we talking about here?

Without context or analysis, this quote stands alone, disconnected from your argument. The reader has to figure out the relevance of the quotation—and the reader can guess wrong. See the fix in the example in the right column.

Tips for Effective Quoting

  • Use signal phrases like “According to” or “Smith argues” to introduce quotes.
  • Blend quotes into your sentences to maintain flow and coherence.
  • Explain the quote’s relevance to your argument immediately after presenting it.
  • Keep quotes concise—use only the most pertinent parts to support your point.

üçîThe Quote Burger: A Three-Part Recipe

Quotations can strengthen your writing—but only if you integrate them smoothly. Use the Quote Burger method to sandwich your quotes between your own words, maintaining your voice and guiding your reader.

Top Bun: Introduce the Quote

Provide context with a signal phrase or narrative lead-in. This introduction prepares the reader for the quote and connects it to your point.

Meat: The Quote Itself

Insert the exact words from your source, enclosed in quotation marks, and include an in-text citation or footnote.

Bottom Bun: Explain the Quote

Follow up with your analysis or commentary, explaining how the quote supports your argument.

Example:

As historian Jane Smith notes, “The revolution began not with a bang, but with a whisper” (Smith 45). This statement suggests that subtle, gradual changes often precede significant societal shifts. We often think changes are huge events that are obvious and significant. In reality, there can be many smaller, barely noticeable changes that happen in the beginning of any cultural or social revolution.

Week 4 Newsletter — Plain Language Focus

Six short article ideas that tie Chapter 5 to students’ research proposals.

1) Write to the Reader: Use “You” for Actions

Show how shifting from “the user/applicant” to “you” clarifies tasks in methods, timelines, and deliverables. Include 2–3 before/after lines students can copy.

2) Shrink Sentences, Sharpen Meaning

Explain the 15–20 word target and quick trims: drop fillers (“in order to”), swap passive for active, cut stacked prepositional phrases. Provide a 3–step micro–edit checklist.

3) Replace Jargon with Plain Terms (or Define on First Use)

Offer a simple “jargon table” pattern: Term → Plain wording → One–line why it matters. Prompt students to build a 5–term mini–glossary for their proposal.

4) Organize for Findability

Model headings, lists, and Q&A blocks so readers can find, understand, and use key info fast. Tie to proposal sections: problem, audience, methods, timeline, ethics.

5) Plain Language as Equity

Connect clarity to access & fairness. Give two concrete checks: define critical terms where decisions happen; write summaries that a non–expert stakeholder can act on.

6) A 10–Minute Plain–Language Tune–Up

A timed mini–workflow students can run before submitting: highlight verbs → convert to active → split long sentences → swap uncommon words → add one locating heading.

Tip for instructors: Each article can include a 3–line “Apply it now” box asking students to paste one before/after sentence from their proposal draft.
