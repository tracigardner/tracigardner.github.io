“ ” Avoid Dropped Quotes

A dropped quote lacks introduction and explanation, leaving readers puzzled.

Let’s say you encounter this quotation in a document:

“The revolution began not with a bang, but with a whisper” (Smith 45).

After reading that, you’re likely to have some questions:

  • What do you mean by a bang?
  • Do you mean an explosion? a loud sound?
  • And a whisper? So it was quiet?
  • What the heck are we talking about here?

Without context or analysis, this quote stands alone, disconnected from your argument. The reader has to figure out the relevance of the quotation—and the reader can guess wrong. See the fix in the example in the right column.

Tips for Effective Quoting

  • Use signal phrases like “According to” or “Smith argues” to introduce quotes.
  • Blend quotes into your sentences to maintain flow and coherence.
  • Explain the quote’s relevance to your argument immediately after presenting it.
  • Keep quotes concise—use only the most pertinent parts to support your point.

üçîThe Quote Burger: A Three-Part Recipe

Quotations can strengthen your writing—but only if you integrate them smoothly. Use the Quote Burger method to sandwich your quotes between your own words, maintaining your voice and guiding your reader.

Top Bun: Introduce the Quote

Provide context with a signal phrase or narrative lead-in. This introduction prepares the reader for the quote and connects it to your point.

Meat: The Quote Itself

Insert the exact words from your source, enclosed in quotation marks, and include an in-text citation or footnote.

Bottom Bun: Explain the Quote

Follow up with your analysis or commentary, explaining how the quote supports your argument.

As historian Jane Smith notes, “The revolution began not with a bang, but with a whisper” (Smith 45). This statement suggests that subtle, gradual changes often precede significant societal shifts.

Advice for Strong Proposals

What separates a decent proposal from a strong, persuasive one? Clear, confident, and credible ideas!

Be Clear About What You’re Explaining

Readers need to understand why your Ut Prosim project matters. How will your explanation serve others and contribute to the common good? Avoid vague claims. Instead, ground your rationale in the scenario, community needs, or relevant research.

Copyright and licensing impact every artist in Maroon + Orange Digital’s Open Artists’ Studio, where collaboration and sharing are part of daily work. Guidance on intellectual property rights and Creative Commons licensing must be clear and accessible, since artists need to know how to protect their own work while legally using or remixing the work of others. If this information is hard to find, both individual artists and the entire studio community may lose opportunities to create and share confidently.

Define Key Terms Clearly

Defining key terms helps your readers follow along and trust your explanation. Don’t assume everyone knows the same words and phrases you do.

Focus on these questions:

  • What key terms will your readers need to understand?
  • How can you define them clearly and briefly, without jargon?
  • What example will make the term useful to your audience?
