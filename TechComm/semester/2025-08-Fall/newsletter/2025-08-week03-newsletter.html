Starting Your Research Proposal

Tomorrow (Monday, 9.8) marks the start of a three-week module devoted to the production of your Research Proposal for the Informational Report.

As with the Subject Selection Email from Module 1, the proposal is connected to the Semester-Long Project — which focuses on a socially-relevant topic connected to your major.

As a reminder, the purpose of the Research Proposal for the Informational Report is to map out your research plan for the report and explain why your topic matters. Presented in memo format, this proposal lays out your project’s scope, research questions, audience analysis, timeline, and initial sources. You’ll also show how your background prepares you to research this topic and describe how your work will support community needs.

Imagine that the proposal is an extended and more complex version of the Subject Selection Email. Although both focus on getting your teacher’s buy-in on your intended topic, they handle the request in different ways.

üò° Annoying Writing Features

Imagine that the proposal is an extended and more complex version of the Subject Selection Email. Although both focus on getting your teacher’s buy-in on your intended topic, they handle the request in different ways.

Your Subject Selection Email

says, “Hey, I have this idea for the project. Here’s why I think it’s a good one, and here’s some info about my intended audience. What do you think about my idea?”

Your Research Proposal for the Informational Report

tells the reader, “Now that you’ve approved my topic, let me show you how I see this project developing, how I’m qualified to research and write about it, and when I will have key parts done. What’s more, here are details about five interesting articles on my topic. Now, have I convinced you that this project will succeed?”

There is one generic proposal sample posted for you on the Document Samples page, but one that is focused more specifically on the actual proposal assignment will be available there by the end of the week.

üìå Strong vs. Annoying Writing: What’s the Bottom Line?

Keep these takeaways in mind not only to avoid common writing pitfalls but also to make your work stronger, more effective, and more professional.

‚úÖ What to Do for Strong Writing

  • Be concise. The top-rated characteristic of strong writing was being short and to the point. Readers appreciate efficiency.
  • Look professional. A well-organized and visually polished document adds credibility to your message.
  • Get the details right. Spelling and grammar mistakes can distract from your message and reduce professionalism.

üö´ What to Avoid in Writing

  • Confusing sentences. The biggest annoyance? Writing that just doesn’t make sense, even when the words are familiar.
  • Careless mistakes. Misspelled names and missing subject lines can make your writing feel unprofessional or rushed.
  • Unreadable formatting. Fonts that are too small or a lack of clear structure can make your writing frustrating to engage with.
