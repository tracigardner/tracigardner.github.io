ü™∂ Writing with Plain Language

Plain style doesn’t mean simple-minded; it means reader-friendly. The key techniques to plain language are the following:

  • Choose short, direct sentences and common words.
  • Replace jargon with everyday terms unless precision requires otherwise.
  • Break up long paragraphs.
  • Use topic sentences.
  • Rely on active verbs.
  • Read aloud—if a sentence feels heavy or abstract, simplify it.

Pick the Right Chart, Not the Pretty One

Match the visualization to the message you want readers to take away in 5 seconds or less.

  • Compare categories: Bar chart (grouped or stacked if needed).
  • Change over time: Line chart (consistent intervals, no broken axes).
  • Part of a whole: Pie or stacked bar (few slices; label clearly).
  • Relationships: Scatter plot (add a trendline only if it clarifies).
  • Magnitude & intensity: Heat map (ordered legend; explain the scale).

Make it readable: Use plain titles that state the takeaway, legible axis labels, and units. Keep grids subtle, legends short, and data-ink high.

Quick test: Cover the chart and read the title + caption‚Äîif the main point isn‚Äôt clear, revise.

From Report to Fact Sheet: What to Keep, What to Cut

Turn a multi-page report into a focused 1‚Äì2 page fact sheet by filtering for essentials:

  • Keep: What the audience needs to know to understand or act (definitions, key facts, one best chart, clear next steps).
  • Cut: Process notes, literature review details, tangents, and anything that doesn‚Äôt serve the audience‚Äôs decision or action.
  • Condense: Replace paragraphs with bullets, tables, and micro-headings; move nuance into concise captions or callouts.
  • Prioritize: Lead with a 2‚Äì3 sentence summary; organize the body into 3‚Äì5 short, headed sections.

Rule of thumb: If a fact doesn‚Äôt earn space on a single page, it doesn‚Äôt belong.

Audience Shift: Leaders ‚Üí Community

Fact sheets serve non-experts who need clarity fast. Adjust your content and tone accordingly.

  • Plain language: Swap jargon for everyday terms; define necessary technical words in-line.
  • Low cognitive load: Short sentences, bullets, icons, and white space for scanning.
  • What they care about: Practical impacts, safety, cost, access, timelines, next steps.
  • Accessibility: Descriptive figure titles, alt-text, and informative captions.

Check: Can a non-expert explain your sheet‚Äôs main point after 30 seconds?

Fast Design Wins (CRAP in 60 Seconds)

Use four quick principles to make your sheet look professional immediately.

  • Contrast: Bold headings vs. body text; emphasize the most important numbers or terms.
  • Repetition: One heading style, one body style, consistent icon set and figure format.
  • Alignment: Snap text and visuals to a clear grid (two columns work well).
  • Proximity: Keep captions next to visuals; group related bullets under clear subheads.

Layout tips: Two columns, concise blocks, limited color palette, and predictable reading flow (F/Z pattern).

Citing on a Single Page (Without Eating the Layout)

  • In-text options: Short author-year in parentheses or superscript numbers that map to a numbered list.
  • References block: A compact list at the bottom or in a sidebar; keep formats consistent.
  • Figure credits: Place source directly in the caption (e.g., Source: CDC, 2024).
  • Only what‚Äôs used: Include sources you actually cite; link or QR code to a longer bibliography if needed.
