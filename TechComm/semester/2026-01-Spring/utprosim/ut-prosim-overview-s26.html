Focusing on Ut Prosim

You probably know a bit about Ut Prosim (That I May Serve), Virginia Tech’s motto.1

When you think about living our motto, you may picture Corps blood drives, the Relay for Life, and The Big Event—but did you know you could fulfill the motto by writing?

When you write documents that help people understand what to do, how to do it, and why it matters, you’re living the Ut Prosim motto.

Here’s how this process works in this course:

  • Ut Prosim is about service, asking you to use your skills and knowledge to help others and improve communities.
  • Technical writing is practical service. It makes information easier to understand and use for people who may be busy, stressed, or unfamiliar with a topic.
  • This course puts Ut Prosim into practice. By writing a series of connected technical writing projects, you’ll help real audiences by making complex information clearer, more usable, and easier to access.

Bottom Line: When technical writing is clear, accessible, and audience-focused, it becomes a way to serve others by making information usable rather than exclusive. That’s how you can fulfill your Ut Prosim motto by writing!

Choosing a Technical Topic That Matters

You’ll choose one technical topic to focus on all semester. Keep these requirements in mind as you choose. Your topic must be:

  • Related to your major: Choose a technical object, process, practice, or idea related to your major that you can research confidently. Check out Report Subject Ideas by Major page for help.
  • Useful in the real world: Connect to a genuine need that people have. Think of topics or things people don‘t understand that affect them.
  • Understandable by for non-expert readers: Select something that you can explain clearly to audiences without any specialized background knowledge.
  • Flexible enough for all six documents: Pick a topic with enough depth and flexibility to fit different audiences, purposes, and formats throughout the semester. See How One Topic Becomes Six Documents for details.
  • Something that interests you: Choose something you want to learn more about—and that you won’t grow tired of before the semester ends!

Why the Sequence Matters

Each project builds on what you’ve already done, helping you deepen your understanding of the topic while improving how you communicate it.

Together, these projects show how technical writing can serve others by making complex information clear, usable, and easier to access.

Step 01

Subject Selection Form

Pick your project subject and fill out the form to tell me about it. Do this for initial approval of your topic, which you will work all semester.

Step 02

Research Proposal

Expand the discussion of your project subject in a formal proposal that includes a literature review. This gives you a quick start on your project research.

Step 03

User Document

Create instructions, a checklist, or a quick-guide-type document to help readers learn helpful how-to information about your subject.

Step 04

Progress Report

Tell your teacher how your work on the informational report is progressing. How much have you gotten done? How much do you have left to do?

Step 05

Informational Report

Write a comprehensive, objective overview of your subject for a group of decision-makers. Tell them enough about it so they can become better informed.

Step 06

Fact Sheet

Create a one-page (front & back) fact sheet to teach the public about your subject in a highly-visual, easy to read way that will appeal to many readers.

Putting It All Together

By the end of the semester, you’ll have created a complete portfolio of technical writing artifacts, all centered around one socially- relevant subject from your field. You’ll have communicated that subject across a variety of professional genres, for multiple audiences, and in multiple formats. You’ll gain experience not only in technical communication, but also in ethical, inclusive, and accessible writing that empowers people to understand and act.

This project is about using your skills to do good work for good reasons. As you research and write, keep asking:

Who benefits from this work? How can I help them understand and use it better?

Your audience — and your community — are counting on you.
