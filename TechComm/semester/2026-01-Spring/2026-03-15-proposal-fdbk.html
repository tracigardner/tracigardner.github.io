Five Ways to Strengthen Your Proposal

1. Name a Specific Decision-Making Group

Avoid broad audiences like “students” or “community members.” Identify a real, concrete body that you could actually name and locate.

Example:
❌ Nope: My audience is the local community.
✔️ Yep! My audience is the Montgomery County School Board.

2. Anchor the Report to One Clear Decision

Your report cannot simply raise awareness or help people understand. It must inform a group that is deciding whether to:

  • Fund something
  • Adopt something
  • Change a policy
  • Implement programming
  • Revise guidelines
  • Approve a project

If there is no identifiable decision, the topic needs reframing.

3. Don’t Write a How-To Guide

This is not instructional writing, a how-to guide, or self-help advice. It is an objective informational report that supports decision-making.

Example:
❌ Nope: Teaching individuals what to do.
✔️ Yep! Explaining systems, structures, impacts, and trade-offs so a group can evaluate options.

4. Stay Objective

Avoid persuasive language (“prove,” “eliminate,” “show why”). Present evidence and let the audience draw conclusions. Be sure you share a balanced, unbiased explanation of the information related to your topic.

Your role is to explain:

  • What it is
  • How it works
  • What evidence shows
  • What its strengths and weaknesses are

5. Get help from the Writing Center

Anyone can visit the Writing Center in the library for help with projects for the course. Writing Center tutors can help you strengthen your content as well as work on document design and other issues. You can read more about the Writing Center and make an appointment on the Writing Center’s webpage.
