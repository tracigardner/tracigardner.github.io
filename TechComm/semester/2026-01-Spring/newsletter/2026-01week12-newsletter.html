üéØ Staying Objective

When your topic involves fairness, accessibility, or community benefit, it’s easy to slip into persuasion.

To stay objective, let the evidence speak. Here’s how:

  • Present multiple expert perspectives.
  • Summarize the experts accurately.
  • Avoid language that implies approval or disapproval.

üí° What Makes a Report Informational (Not Persuasive)

What makes a report informational rather than persuasive? Table 1 summarizes the key differences.

Table 1. Informational Report vs. Persuasive Report
Informational Report Persuasive Report
Answers the question “What is this subject all about?” Answers the question “Why should we support it?”
Explains, defines, and analyzes. Recommends or argues.
Aims to inform readers. Aim to influence decisions.
Presents verified facts, shows contrasting expert views, and uses neutral transitions. Makes calls to action and evaluative statements.

üß© Defining Terms for Non-Experts

Even common terms can confuse readers outside your field. Try these strategies to make sure your readers understand:

  • Add brief internal definitions the first time you use a technical term—for example, “carbon capture, a process that removes carbon dioxide from the air.”
  • Avoid long glossary-style explanations; instead, integrate short clarifications naturally into your sentences.
  • If you must use specialized terms, define them in plain language and relate them to familiar ideas.

ü™∂ Writing with Plain Language

Plain style doesn’t mean simple-minded; it means reader-friendly. The key techniques to plain language are the following:

  • Choose short, direct sentences and common words.
  • Replace jargon with everyday terms unless precision requires otherwise.
  • Break up long paragraphs.
  • Use topic sentences.
  • Rely on active verbs.
  • Read aloud—if a sentence feels heavy or abstract, simplify it.
