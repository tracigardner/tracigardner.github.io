Test Usability with Your Roommates

You don’t need a formal lab or survey to test your user document—just someone nearby. A roommate, friend, or classmate can help you see how a real reader experiences your user document.

1. Set Up Your Test

  • Prepare your materials: Have your draft ready to share in print or on a screen.
  • Give a brief scenario: For example, “Imagine you’re trying to set up this software for the first time.”
  • Stay quiet: Let them read and act on your document without your help. Your goal is to observe, not teach.

2. Observe Carefully

  • Watch for hesitation, rereading, or skipped steps—these are usability red flags.
  • Notice how they respond to headings, visuals, and layout cues.
  • Take notes on what seems confusing, crowded, or out of order.

3. Ask Reflective Questions

  • What parts were clear or helpful?
  • Where did you have to stop and think?
  • What would make this easier to follow?

4. Revise with Design in Mind

Use what you learned to improve readability and flow. Try:

  • Adding white space and headings to guide the eye.
  • Revising unclear steps or adding visuals where users hesitated.
  • Reordering information so the most important actions come first.

Specific Subject Lines

The subject line of a memo is your reader’s first clue to its purpose. A strong subject line helps busy readers understand what the memo covers and why it matters.

  • Be specific, not vague. “Meeting Update” is too general. Try “New Schedule for Weekly Team Meetings” instead.
  • Use keywords that summarize the topic. Readers often search or file memos by subject line, so make sure yours is searchable and clear.
  • Indicate purpose or action when possible. Phrases like “Request for,” “Recommendation on,” or “Summary of” tell readers what to expect.
  • Write the subject line last. After you’ve drafted the memo, it’s easier to capture its main message accurately and concisely.

User Document FAQ

Here are the answers to the questions submitted in last week’s Check-in Survey. Click on the question to reveal the answer.

The audience for our user document should be different from our informational report, right?

Yes. The informational report is written for a non-expert audience of decisionmakers. Your User Document is written for non-expert users from the local community—such as new users, community members, caregivers, students, or volunteers.

The difference is that the members of your User Document audience are not decisionmakers. They are most likely to be the people who will be affected by the decision that the readers of your Informational Report for Non-Expert Readers. See the Choosing Your User Document Topic Announcement for some examples.

How can I create an instruction set for a comparison topic?

This question is hard to answer without some more information. In general, you can choose a focus that relates to one option or the other that you are comparing. If this is your question, send me an email with more details on your topic.

How should we format the User Document?

In addition to the formatting information in the assignment, you can use the How to Prepare an American Omelet example as a model for the formatting.

Is this going to be very similar to the Fact Sheet near the end of the course?

No, the Fact Sheet is a completely different genre of writing from step-by-step instructions for the User Document assignment.

I am unsure about the audience. My audience is young professionals, and I got marked down for the assignment because it was too broad. I am unsure how this is too broad when the audience is aged 22–29.

I think this question relates to the Subject Selection Memo, rather than the User Document.

The issue isn’t the age range. Even though 22–29 narrows the demographic, “young professionals” is still a broad population rather than a specific decision-making group. For the Informational Report, your audience must be a concrete body that can be named and located—such as a particular board, committee, office, or organization that is responsible for making a decision.

For example, instead of writing to “young professionals aged 22–29,” you might write to:

  • The HR leadership team at a specific company
  • The board of a local Chamber of Commerce
  • A university financial wellness office
  • A nonprofit serving early-career professionals
