Fast Design Wins

Good design doesn‚Äôt have to be complicated. Stick to two columns, use one or two fonts, and keep plenty of white space. Repeat styles so the page feels unified, not busy. Keep visuals close to the text they explain and use a limited color palette for emphasis. Your layout should guide the eye naturally—top left to bottom right—without clutter or distraction. Clean, consistent, and easy to scan will always impress more than decorative overload.

Smart Source Citing on a Small Page

Even on a single page, readers deserve to know where your facts come from. Keep citations light but clear: a short author-date reference or a small numbered list works fine. Put image or chart credits right in the caption (for example, Source: CDC, 2025). If you have many sources, add a compact reference block at the bottom or link to a full list online. Citations protect your credibility without stealing your layout‚Äôs breathing room.

Fast Design Wins (CRAP in 60 Seconds)

Use four quick principles to make your sheet look professional immediately.

  • Contrast: Bold headings vs. body text; emphasize the most important numbers or terms.
  • Repetition: One heading style, one body style, consistent icon set and figure format.
  • Alignment: Snap text and visuals to a clear grid (two columns work well).
  • Proximity: Keep captions next to visuals; group related bullets under clear subheads.

Layout tips: Two columns, concise blocks, limited color palette, and predictable reading flow (F/Z pattern).

Citing on a Single Page (Without Eating the Layout)

Credit sources clearly while preserving space.

  • In-text options: Short author-year in parentheses or superscript numbers that map to a numbered list.
  • References block: A compact list at the bottom or in a sidebar; keep formats consistent.
  • Figure credits: Place source directly in the caption (e.g., Source: CDC, 2024).
  • Only what‚Äôs used: Include sources you actually cite; link or QR code to a longer bibliography if needed.
