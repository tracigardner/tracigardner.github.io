Documentation Options

Depending upon the system that you choose, you may use footnotes, endnotes, or in-text citations.

These three options differ in the information they include and in where they belong in the document. The exact details depend upon the documentation system you’re using. That said, here are the major differences to keep in mind:

Table 2. Documentation Options
Documentation Option Information Included Location of the Information
Footnotes The name(s) of the author(s), source title, and relevant page number or similar details. Footnote number immediately follows the relevant information in the document. Documentation information is at the bottom (the foot) of the page where the reference is mentioned.
Endnotes The name(s) of the author(s), source title, and relevant page number or similar details. Endnote number immediately after relevant information in the text of the document. Documentation information is on a separate page (or pages), at the end of a document, in a numbered list in the order they appear in the document.
In-Text Citations The name(s) of the author(s), sometimes the year of publication, and often the relevant page number or similar details. Typically in parentheses. Example: (Tyson, 2025). Either immediately after the reference in the document or at the end of the sentence (or other passage) that refers to the source.

Questions about Dates

Most of you indicated that you understand the system for Target Due Dates, the Grace Period, and the Revision Deadline.

Here are the answers to the questions you asked in your Check-In Survey feedback.

I submitted a revision on time last week, but it still says late/Incomplete.
I haven’t had a chance to check your revision yet. Once I do, I’ll leave a comment and either update your score OR ask you to revise again.
Is the revision deadline 6 days after the target due date, or do you set them based on when you send out the grades?
In my perfect little world, the Revision Deadline is 6 days after the Target Due Date. In the real world, I may change it if I think you’ll need more time to get your revision done. Basically, I don’t want my inability to get grades to you fast enough to hurt you. Everyone deserves the change to revise.
Is there a downside to always using the grace period?
Yes. If you rely on the grace period every week, you may fall behind in the course schedule and find it harder to keep up with new work.
What counts as a “good-faith effort” on a major project?
Your submission must respond to the assignment and meet basic requirements such as topic, focus, length, and format.
Can I revise a major project multiple times?
Yes. As long as your initial submission was on time (by the end of the grace period) and met good-faith effort, you may revise as often as you like until the revision deadline.
Why does Canvas show Late label on my work?
This is an automatic label. It does not affect your grade. There is no Late penalty in this course. If you do not turn your work in by the end of the Grace Period however, you’ll ean an Incomplete.
Why is the final exam different?
The final exam does not have a grace period because exam week already provides extended time. You may complete it anytime during exam week, up to the last moment of the last day of exam week (11:59 PM on Wednesday 17).

Notices in Instructions

Notices are short, clearly labeled statements that alert readers to risks, prevent mistakes, or offer helpful guidance right before (or beside) a relevant step.

Step-by-step instructions often include notices—but each type has a specific purpose. Use them correctly.

 Danger

Use Danger when there is risk of death or severe injury.

 Warning

Use Warning when there is risk of harm or major damage.

 Caution

Use Caution when there is possible risk.

Note

Use Note to provide helpful tips, clarification, or troubleshooting guidance.

These are not interchangeable labels. They signal the level of risk to your reader. If everything is labeled “Warning,” then nothing stands out.

  • Does my task involve real safety risks?
  • Am I overstating danger?
  • Would a “Note” be more appropriate than a “Caution”?
  • Is the notice placed immediately before the risky step?

Clear notices increase credibility and usability. Misused notices reduce trust.

Be precise. Your readers are counting on it.
