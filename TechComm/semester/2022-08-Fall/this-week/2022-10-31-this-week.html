Why Your Report Outline Matters

One glance 👀 at your outline tells me if you understand the format for the Recommendation Report. In fact, I can usually predict whether your report will earn a Complete just by the details in your outline.

Here’s what you should include in your report outline:

  • A clear, specific title for your report.
  • All of the required sections of your report.
  • Specific subheadings that organize your sections.

Focus on creating parallel structures that lead the reader through the report in a clear, organized fashion.

Find the list of required sections on the Recommendation Report Criteria page. Check the table of contents in the Sample Report, Figure 18.8 in Markel & Selber, for examples of strong subheadings.

Quoting, Paraphrasing, & Summarizing

Your responses on the Week 10 Check-In Survey tell me that most of you (74%) are comfortable with integrating quotations in your writing. That’s great!

You were less accurate with your understanding of paraphrasing versus summarizing. Basically paraphrasing is putting ideas from a source into your own words. If you include any exact phrases from the original, you put them in quotation marks, and you always add a citation of your source. Summarizing, on the other hand, is a brief version of the original that includes the main ideas only. Again, you need to include a citation of your source.

The How We Cite video on the left (from the UNC Writing Center) provides a quick review of quoting, paraphrasing, summarizing, and generalizing. The video will take you less than two minutes and tell you everything you need to do, including when you need citations and quotation marks.

If you are worried about plagiarism, check out the tip sheet from UNC for more details. For more details on using quotations, visit their Quotations page.

What You’re Worried About 😟

I asked you to tell me what you were worried about as you began work on your Recommendation Report. Your responses fell into three unsurprising categories: running out of time, writing such a long document, and findig the research that you need to support your idea.

Those categories are unsurprising to me because they are the same worries students have had in my previous courses. More importantly, most students do just fine, despite these concerns. Here are some tips to help you beat the challenges.

The Time Available 🕛

It’s true. And you need write (and possible revise) your report by the end of the Grace Period.

The key is to space out your writing during the weekdays before Thanksgiving. We have about 15 weekdays before break. Do a section of the report each day, and you should have a draft by November 18.

Another tip is to follow the step-by-step instruction pages on the Tip Sheet. Schedule one of those instructions pages to follow for each weekday for an easy plan to get that work done!

The Report’s Length 📃

Longer documents worry people. Short documents are hard enough, but a long document? That can raise anxiety levels for even the best writer.

The Recommendation Report is a long document. Your report will probably be 10–15 single-spaced pages (though remember there is not a minimum or maximum length).

Here’s a secret: count the number of sections and items you need to include in your front matter, body, and back matter. If each of those sections begins on a new page, you’ll easily reach ten or more pages. Just fill in those pages.

The Research 📚

You need primary and secondary research to support the recommendations in your report.

The good news is the research from your Pitch also works for your report. If you still need information, take advantage of these resources:

  • Librarians love to help students with research questions. You just have to ask them.
  • Your friends and classmates can answer a simple survey if you need student opinions.
  • Look for government websites that address the issues you are examining. Lots of agencies address food, health, and nutrition.
