Write Emails People Want to Read

Email is still the backbone of professional communication, but inboxes are crowded and attention spans are short. If you want your message to stand out (in a good way), you need to think about more than just grammar and spelling—you need a strategy.

Effective emails are not long lectures or confusing puzzles. They’re quick to understand, easy to navigate, and respectful of the reader’s time.

Key Principles for Smarter Email Writing

  • Start strong: Your subject line should tell the reader what they will get by opening the email. Be clear, not clever.
  • Think mobile-first: Most emails today are read on a phone. If your main point doesn’t fit in the first few lines, many people won’t scroll down to find it.
  • Front-load important information: Don’t bury key details deep in the email. Assume your reader might skim, not study.
  • Use visual structure: Break up long text into short sections, bullets, or bolded headings. Make it easy to scan and digest.
  • Include a clear action: If you need something, say exactly what and when. Avoid vague requests like “let me know what you think” unless that truly is your goal.
  • Be human, but professional: Use a friendly tone when appropriate, but skip the filler ("I hope this email finds you well!") unless it really fits the situation.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Paragraph walls: Dense blocks of text make people want to click away.
  • Mystery subject lines: If the reader can’t tell what the email is about from the subject line, they might not open it.
  • Unclear requests: Readers should never have to guess what you need from them.

Why This Matters

Writing better emails isn’t just about being polite. It’s about showing that you respect the reader’s time and understand how professional communication works. Strong email habits can build trust, strengthen teamwork, and even open doors to future opportunities.

Email That Works

Email is a noisy environment. Stand out. Be brief, bold, and clear. Think outside the paragraph.

Tips for Effective Email

  1. Revealing subject line: Tell readers what to expect. Also becomes an accurate heading for reply threads.
  2. Microwave greeting: If you must introduce yourself, do it simply and quickly to warm things up.
  3. Use “I” and “you”: Use pronouns to be direct and involve the reader.
  4. Summary sentences: Use the first 20 words to tell readers what to expect.
  5. Skimmable headings and bullets: Avoid long paragraphs. Use formatting features to aid skimming.
  6. Links: Let readers click to review detailed material rather than overloading the email with information.
  7. Graphics: Use photos or charts to clarify or support your content.
  8. Call to action: Be clear about what you want the reader to do next.
  9. Deadline: Let readers know how much time they have to respond or act.
  10. 250-word limit: Be brief for maximum effectiveness, especially on a phone screen.

Sample Email Example

From: Michonne Z. Slayer, VP of HR
To: All staff
Re: A few steps to prepare for the zombie apocalypse

The company has launched its zombie apocalypse preparedness plan (ZAPP).

As your HR leader, I’ve prepared this email to explain what actions you should take.

Why now?

After the well-publicized zombie takeover in Fayetteville, Georgia, the CEO and I created this preparedness plan to ensure that our employees will be safe and productive in the event of zombie attacks.

Learn about the zombie threat

If zombies make you nervous, it’s completely understandable. To be properly prepared, review the materials below:

  • CDC’s zombie apocalypse web site
  • ZAPP directory on our corporate intranet
  • ZAPP group on our internal social network (for updates)

What you should do

  • We’re seeking volunteers in each office to sign up as ZAPP captains and take our short online training course. See your local HR leader by the end of this month.
  • In the event of an attack, find the volunteers in your office — they’ll have red ZAPP caps at their desks.
  • One break room in each office now has a ZAPP kit, including fresh water, tools, first-aid supplies, and crossbows. Learn the location of the ZAPP kit in your office.
  • Please be aware that our health plan does not cover zombieism, and that bereavement policies do not apply to the undead.

I know it’s a busy time of year, but please review these resources and policies this week and, if you’re so inclined, volunteer to help. Let’s support each other and stay safe.
