Integrating Graphics Without Breaking Your Flow

Images and graphics can make your report easier to understand—but only if they’re placed and explained clearly. A strong graphic supports your point. A misplaced one distracts from it.

Visuals should work with your words, not interrupt them. Guide your reader to and through each image.

This post will help you add visuals smoothly, keep your layout clean, and wrap your writing around graphics without losing your reader.

Don’t just drop an image in at random—put it next to the paragraph that explains or introduces it. Readers should see a visual right when they need it.

Example: If you’re discussing campus lighting savings, place the energy savings chart right after the sentence that introduces it.

Don’t assume readers will know what the image shows. Briefly tell them what it is and why it matters.

For smaller visuals (like icons or photos), you can float them to one side to make your layout more interesting. Keep enough padding so the text doesn’t crowd the image.

For example, this photo of a man installing a light could float to the right of a paragraph discussing the environmental benefits of energy-efficient lighting.

Use this strategy when the graphic supports the main idea but doesn’t need its own section.

üìê Keep Alignment and Spacing Clean

Make sure images are aligned with text or margins and have breathing room. Misaligned or tightly packed graphics can make your page feel cluttered.

  • Use even margins on all sides of the image.
  • Don’t center large graphics between two blocks of text. Instead anchor them in context.
  • Always label figures and refer to them in your writing.
