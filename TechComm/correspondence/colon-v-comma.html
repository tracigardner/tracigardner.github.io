After the Salutation, Who Wins?

When you write a salutation in a letter or email (e.g., “Dear X”), you must follow it with one punctuation mark. The matchup is simple:

Colon = Formal correspondence Comma = Informal correspondence

Think about your audience and the situation. If the context is official, evaluative, or you’re addressing a role or office, the colon takes the round. If the tone is friendly or familiar, the comma gets the nod.

Formal (Use a colon)

  • Dear Hiring Committee:
  • Dear Professor Nguyen:
  • Dear Dr. Patel:
  • Dear Members of the Board:
  • Dear Admissions Office:

Informal (Use a comma)

  • Hi, Sam,
  • Hello, everyone,
  • Hey, Jamie,
  • Dear Mom,
  • Hi, team,
Note: In the informal examples, notice the comma after Hi, Hello, and Hey—and the comma after the name/group, too.

Quick Ringside Test

If you’d sign off with your full name and a title, or you’re writing to an office/committee, choose the colon. If you’d sign with just your first name and the vibe is conversational, choose the comma.

After the Salutation, Who Wins?

When you use a salutation (e.g., Dear X) in a letter or email, you must decide whether you want to follow that salutation with a comma or a colon. You must use one, so let’s figure it out.

The matchup is simple:

  • Use a colon after a salutation in formal correspondence. (Formal=colon)
  • Use a comma after a salutation in informal correspondence. (Informal=comma)

How do you know when a piece of correspondence is formal? Informal? Consider your audience and the situation.

Formal Correspondence Examples

      • Dear Hiring Committee: (Job application)
      • Dear Professor Nguyen: (Academic inquiry)
      • Dear Dr. Patel: (Medical or research-related correspondence)
      • Dear Members of the Board: (Business or organizational matters)
      • Dear Admissions Office: (College or university application)

Informal Correspondence Examples

      • Hi, Sam, (Text or email to a friend)
      • Hello, everyone, (Group message to classmates)
      • Hey, Jamie, (Casual note or email)
      • Dear Mom, (Personal letter)
      • Hi, team, (Informal workplace message)

Notice the comma after Hi, Hello, and Hey!

Headings: Sizing & Styling

Although you are not required to use headings for the Scope & Feasibility and Audience Analysis sections of your email, you certainly can. If you do, however, you should follow one guidelines laid out in the document design chapter of our textbook:

No heading should be the same size as the body text.

You'll read more about doc design later, but what this means for the email is this: Since all headings need to stand out, if you use them for this email, and your body text is 12 pts, they should be at least 14 pts and bold. You'll be able to see right away how much more visible they become!

Please note that you should not use a heading for the introduction or conclusion.
