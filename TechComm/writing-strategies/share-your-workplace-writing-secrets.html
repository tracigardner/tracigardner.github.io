Most of you come to this class with workplace experience. You may have worked in an internship or job in your field. You may have experience working in a job here on campus. You may have worked in an after-school or summer job while you were in high school. You may have experience watching a family member or friend in the workplace. 

No matter what experience you have, you have seen documents in the workplace—everything from posters on the bulletin boards to contracts, and from letters to proposals. You may have even written some documents yourself, like an email to your supervisor asking for a day off or a memo that provided your team with an update on your work. 

Your experience has surely taught you some tricks and strategies to survive as a writer in the workplace. You might think about these questions as you consider what you already know about writing on the job: 

  • Where do you start?
  • How do you do it? What techniques do you use?
  • How do you keep everything organized?
  • How do you remember how to incorporate details and evidence? 
  • What can you do to make sure people read what you write?

As a way to introduce yourself to the class, write a reply that tells us about one of the tricks you have learned or developed. In your post, tell us the following: 

  • your major and career goal (i.e., what do you want to be when you graduate).
  • your secret for writing in the workplace.
  • how you learned your secret and/or how you figured out that it was effective.

 

This question is adapted from Scott Warnock’s Teaching Writing Online: How & Why (2009), pp. 98–99.
