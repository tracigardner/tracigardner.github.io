Students often start with topics that are interesting and meaningful—but a little too broad, too instructional, or aimed at the wrong audience. For the report you are proposing, you’re not just explaining a subject. You’re writing to a specific, non-expert decision-making group that is evaluating a clear choice.

The examples in Table 1 (below) show how you can shift from a general awareness topic to a focused, decision-centered report. Notice how the revised versions make these changes:

  • They narrow the audience.
  • They define a concrete decision.
  • They maintain an objective tone.

Topic & Audience Examples

❌ Don’t Use This

I plan to write about carbon capture technology and how it can help fight climate change. My audience will be people who care about the environment.

⚠️ Why It’s Wrong

The audience is vague and not a decision-making body, and the purpose leans toward advocacy rather than objective analysis.

✅ Try This Instead

I will analyze how carbon capture technology functions, its documented costs and limitations, and its impact on municipal energy infrastructure for the Roanoke City Council as they evaluate whether to partner with a regional carbon capture pilot program.

❌ Don’t Use This

I want to focus on student loan debt and help recent graduates understand how to pay it off faster. My audience will be young adults with student loans.

⚠️ Why It’s Wrong

This reads like a how-to guide, and the audience is a general population rather than a specific group making a program decision.

✅ Try This Instead

I will examine federal student loan repayment structures, income-driven repayment plans, and long-term cost projections for the Hokie Wellness’s Financial Wellness Office as they consider expanding post-graduation loan counseling services.

❌ Don’t Use This

I want to focus on remote work and explain why it is beneficial for employees and companies. My audience will be working professionals.

⚠️ Why It’s Wrong

The purpose is persuasive (“why it is beneficial”), and the audience is too broad and not tied to a clear decision-making group.

✅ Try This Instead

I will analyze productivity data, employee retention trends, cybersecurity implications, and infrastructure costs related to hybrid work models for the executive leadership team of Brown Edwards (a Roanoke, Virginia accounting firm) as they determine whether to formalize a long-term hybrid work policy.

❌ Don’t Use This

I want to write about cybersecurity threats and explain how small businesses can protect themselves from hackers. My audience will be small business owners.

⚠️ Why It’s Wrong

This version is instructional and lacks a specific governing body responsible for deciding whether to implement training.

✅ Try This Instead

I will evaluate common cybersecurity vulnerabilities, documented breach costs, and available training models for the board of the Capital Region Small Business Development Center as they decide whether to sponsor annual cybersecurity workshops for member businesses.

❌ Don’t Use This

I plan to focus on electric vehicle charging stations and explain why communities need more of them. My audience will be drivers and community members.

⚠️ Why It’s Wrong

The topic argues for a solution and names a general public audience rather than a specific governing body making an infrastructure decision.

✅ Try This Instead

I will analyze installation costs, grid capacity considerations, usage data, and funding mechanisms for the Blacksburg Town Council as they consider whether to expand public electric vehicle charging infrastructure.
