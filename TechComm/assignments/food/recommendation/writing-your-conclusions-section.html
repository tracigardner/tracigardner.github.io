Overview

The Conclusions section tells your readers what your research data means. You discuss the implications of your findings and explain how your interpretation can influence how to proceed based on your research.

Where You Can Find Help

  • From Markel & Selber, Chapter 18:
    • The details on the Conclusions section, from p. 494:
      “Conclusions answer the question ‘What does it mean?’ They are the implications of the results. To draw conclusions, you need to think carefully about your results, weighing whether they point clearly to a single meaning.”
    • Example Conclusions section, on pp. 523–524 (part of the sample recommendation report in Figure 18.8).
    • “Evaluating the Information,” on pp. 132–35 (in Chapter 6 of the textbook).
  • LinkedIn Learning video, “Conclusions and recommendations” (4m 9s), from the Technical Writing: Reports course.

How You Do It

  1. Complete a draft of your Methods and Results sections before beginning this section. The interpretation in this section should directly connect to the information in the two previous sections of the report.

  3. Write the content for the Conclusions section:
    1. Explain the implications of your research under each of the sub-headings.
    2. Refer to the research results to support your implications.
    3. Work back through the information you have added to make sure you have sentences and clear ideas.
    4. Focus this section on your conclusions only. Save your recommendations for the Recommendations section.
    5. Ensure that any information you quote from your primary or secondary research is enclosed in quotation marks. Examples might be something that someone says in a response to a survey or interview (both primary research) or something stated in an journal article or book (both secondary research).
    6. Add Documentation and Citations anywhere that you include paraphrased information or quotations from outside sources.
  5. Compare the subheadings you have included with the tasks you have listed elsewhere in the document. Ensure that they all use the same phrasing.
  6. Review your Conclusions section to make sure that you have included everything that answers the question, “What did it [your methods and results] mean?”
  7. Move on to the next part of the report that you want to work on.
