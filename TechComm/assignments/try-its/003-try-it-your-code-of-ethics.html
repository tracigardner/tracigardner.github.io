Question 1

Find the codes of ethic for your intended profession. Most disciplines will have several. For example, a quick Google search for "Code of ethics for engineers" presented over 3 million hits. So narrow your search. Instead of engineering, try electrical engineering. Here are some examples:

  • An electrical engineer would probably focus on the IEEE Code of Ethics.
  • A biologist might focus on the Code of Ethics for the Society for Conservation Biology.

Once you identify the principles for your field, do the following:

  1. Find the code of ethics for your profession.
  2. Read it.
  3. In a thoughtful response of 150-200 words (75 pts), consider the following questions:
    • Have you read this before?
    • Have the professors in your discipline referred to this in class?
    • What struck you as important or noteworthy about this code?
    • Does it align with any of the definitions in the ethics chapter of our text?
    • Have you seen this code in practice -- for example in an internship or co-op or summer employment?
  • Where do you find principles explicitly related to writing or communication in the code of ethics for your field?
  • Where are connections less obvious?
  • Are there ideas about writing and communication that you think they are missing?

After you analyze the code for your field, share what you have found in your submission, including this information:

  • Identify your field and the relevant ethical code.
  • Provide a link to your ethical code.
  • Talk about what you found about writing and communication in your field’s ethical code. What makes something an ethical document in your field?

Canvas is set so that you must post your response before seeing the response of others.
