How You Do It

  1. Return to your responses to the Brainstorming Your Intro activity (You can find it on the Grades page) and skim through your responses.
  2. If you skipped any questions, answer them now. You should have a response for every one of the nine questions.
  3. Open the word processor document where you are working on your recommendation report.
  4. Scroll through the outline you created to the location for your Introduction section.
  5. Write the content for the Methods Section:
    1. Copy the information from the Brainstorming Your Intro activity, and paste it into your recommendation report document.

      2. Writing Clear, Informative Paragraphs

      Because you are working from the Q&A notes from the Brainstorming Your Intro activity, you need to ensure that your introduction flows smoothly. The information needs to sound like paragraphs, rather than a series of answers to questions.

      Use these sections from the textbook to as you compose and revise your introduction:

      • Use the advice in the “Structure Paragraphs Clearly” section, from Chapter 9 of the textbook (pp. 202–207).
      • See the strategies in the “Use Coherence Devices within and between Paragraphs” section, from Chapter 9 of the textbook (pp. 207–210)
    2. Use the details in Chapter 7, “Organizing Your Information,” to determine how you want to structure your information. It is possible to use more than one pattern. For instance, you might begin with a general-to-specific pattern or a chronological pattern to provide basic information and background, and then you might shift to a problem-methods-solution pattern to provide an overview the report.
    3. Rearrange your notes from the Brainstorming Your Intro activity to fit the pattern(s) you have chosen.
    4. Work back through the information you have added to expand the notes into full paragraphs.
    5. Revise your introduction to make sure you have transitions, sentences and clear ideas.
    6. Add Documentation and Citations anywhere that you include paraphrased information or quotations from outside sources.
    7. Ensure that any information you quote from your primary or secondary research is enclosed in quotation marks. Examples might be something that someone says in a response to a survey or interview (both primary research) or something stated in an journal article or book (both secondary research).
  6. Review the section and make any additions or changes, using the information from the textbook and LinkedIn Learning video as needed. At this point, focus in particular in making sure that you have included everything that answers the question, “What is this document about?”
