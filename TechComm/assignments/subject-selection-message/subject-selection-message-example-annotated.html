Scope & Feasibility

I chose this topic because young artists regularly create and share work online but often do not understand copyright or licensing. As a result, they may give up rights to their work or avoid sharing it altogether out of concern about misuse.

I plan to address questions such as the following:

  • What rights do creators automatically have when they produce original work?
  • How does copyright apply to digital art, music, and writing created by teens?
  • What options do Creative Commons licenses provide, and how do those choices affect sharing and reuse?

This topic is narrow enough to manage while still allowing for clear, practical explanations for non-expert readers.

Audience Analysis

The primary audience for the informational report will be the Board of Directors for a teen arts studio program. Board members are experienced in arts education and program oversight, but they are not legal experts. They may be familiar with copyright in general terms but need guidance on how it applies to youth-created digital work.

This audience needs clear definitions, concrete examples, and simple comparisons to support policy and program decisions. To meet these needs, I will use plain language, short sections, and visuals such as tables. I will also include realistic examples that reflect how teens create and share work online.
