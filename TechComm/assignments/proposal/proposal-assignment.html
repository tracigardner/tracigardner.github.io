What I Want You to Do

You and the members of your group will compose a proposal to write a recommendation report for improvements to a website, chosen by the group. Each group member will share their Usability Revision and Reflection with the members of your group, and then the group will choose one of the sites to focus on.

In addition to telling me about the site your groups has chosen, your proposal will provide a schedule for your work on the recommendation report, including plans for group meetings and draft deadlines. It will also identify the responsibilities of all group members.

Why I Want You to Do It

For our course, I want you to provide your group’s topic and plan for the Recommendation Report. Your proposal will give me the chance to help you are you move on to writing your Recommendation Report as well as give your group the opportunity to make preliminary decisions that will be important to your success.

Beyond this course, research proposals are common features in academia. Before a PhD, MA, or MFA candidate begins work on a dissertation or thesis, for example, they will submit to a committee of readers a well-developed plan for that research document. Additionally, in many technical and scientific courses—but even in humanities ones—an upper-division research project is often preceded by such a proposal.

  4. Use the information in the readings and below to revise your user documentation and write your Reflection Memo:

    User Documentation Revision

    1. Make a copy of the user documentation that you have chosen by copying the text and pasting it into your word processor.
    2. Revise and edit the user documentation in your word processor, using these instructions:
      • Writing
        Whenever you find convoluted, overly formal, and confusing language, simplify it.
      • Design
        Apply design principles (CRAP, color, white space, etc.). Use a title and internal headings, as well as lists (when needed).
      • Length
        As long as it needs to be in order to be an easy-to-read, easy-to-use document.
      • Ethical Considerations
        Revise any content that misleads, pressures, or coerces the reader into thinking or doing something. Examine how people with disabilities are talked about, and adjust if necessary. Consider whether the content is inclusive, and broaden it as needed.
      • Legal Considerations
        Ensure disclaimers and policies are clear and legible. Verify that citations are used properly in the document.
      • Cultural Considerations
        Are there multiple readers of this document? Does the content need to be modified for international readers? Are there cultural issues that could be addressed or avoided? (Respond to these and more, if applicable to your chosen document.)
    3. Check and update your revised document with the review tools in your word processor, using the following links if you need instructions:
      • Check spelling and grammar in Office
      • Check grammar, spelling, and more in Word for the web (for Microsoft 365)
      • Check your spelling & grammar in Google Docs
      • Check spelling in Pages on Mac
    4. Check and update your revised user documentation for accessibility, using the following links if you need instructions:
      • Improve accessibility with the Accessibility Checker (for Office and for Microsoft 365)
      • Accessibility for Google Docs, Sheets, Slides, & Drawings
      • Create accessible documents, spreadsheets, or presentations with Pages, Numbers, or Keynote
    5. Save your document as a PDF to ensure that your document’s layout and design are not changed when you upload the file to Canvas.
    6. Open and check that your PDF shows the document design you intend before submitting the file.
