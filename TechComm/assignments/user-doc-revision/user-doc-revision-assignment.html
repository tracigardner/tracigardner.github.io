What I Want You to Do

This assignment consists of two parts:

  1. You will revise existing user documentation to improve its usability. In other words, you will use plain language and document design principles to make the existing document easier for readers to use as they complete the task it focuses on.
  2. You will discuss the changes you make to the user documentation in a reflection memo. Your memo will analyze the user document you revised and the changes you made to it in order to improve its usability.

Why I Want You to Do It

Effective technical writing focuses on meeting the needs of its readers, the audience for the document. The tech writing jargon for this function is ensuring a positive user experience, or UX. In this assignment, you will practice how to design/redesign existing user documentation in order to improve its usability, giving the reader a more positive user experience.

The activity also gives you the opportunity to apply technical writing strategies of plain language and document design, two techniques that will be important in future major projects in the course.

How You Do It

  1. Review the User Documentation Revision & Reflection Memo Criteria to understand the requirements for the project.
  2. Read Chapter 17: Instructions from the Howdy or Hello? textbook to familiarize yourself with the characteristics of user documentation. Chapter 17 sections are Writing Instructions, Preliminary Steps, Common Sections in Instructions, Writing Style, and Illustrating Your Instructions.
  3. Choose the user documentation that you will analyze and revise:
    • Pick a webpage or PDF with instructions that someone in your intended career would use to complete a work task.
    • Select a webpage or PDF that you are familiar with to make the project easier.
    • Choose a webpage or PDF that you can improve in terms of usability.
    • Do not choose any webpage or PDF that I (Traci) have written. That gets weird.
    Some example documents you can use are as follows:
    • instructions for an RFP, grant application, or government form.
    • online help files or troubleshooting documents.
    • directions to access or complete a procedure, such as a section of a user manual or user guide.
  4. Make a backup of the user documentation you choose. Websites and online files can change frequently. By saving a backup, you’ll avoid the situation where the page changes while you are drafting. If that happens, the information in your project (the answers you provide) can change. Here are some ways to make a backup:
    • Print out the webpage on paper, ensuring all the information on the page prints.
    • Print the webpage and choose to print a PDF, so you’ll have a digital backup.
    • Save a backup to the Wayback Machine by following these instructions.
  5. Use the information in the readings and below to revise your user documentation and write your Reflection Memo:

    User Documentation Revision

    1. Make a copy of the user documentation that you have chosen by copying the text and pasting it into your word processor.
    2. Search for any brand guidelines that indicate company or organization standards that should apply to the user documentation. for example, Virginia Tech websites follow the VT Brand Guidelines. Use the brand guidelines as you make design choices in your revision.
    3. Revise and edit the user documentation in your word processor, using these instructions:
      • Writing
        Whenever you find convoluted, overly formal, and confusing language, simplify it.
      • Design
        Apply design principles (CRAP, color, white space, etc.). Use a title and internal headings, as well as lists (when needed).
      • Length
        As long as it needs to be to make it an easy-to-read, easy-to-use document.
      • Ethical Considerations
        Revise any content that misleads, pressures, or coerces the reader into thinking or doing something. Examine how people with disabilities are talked about, and adjust if necessary. Consider whether the content is inclusive, and broaden it as needed.
      • Legal Considerations
        Ensure disclaimers and policies are clear and legible. Verify that citations are used properly in the document.
      • Cultural Considerations
        Are there multiple readers of this document? Does the content need to be modified for international readers? Are there cultural issues that could be addressed or avoided? (Respond to these and more, if applicable to your chosen document.)
    4. Check and update your revised document with the review tools in your word processor, using the following links if you need instructions:
      • Check spelling and grammar in Office
      • Check grammar, spelling, and more in Word for the web (for Microsoft 365)
      • Check your spelling & grammar in Google Docs
      • Check spelling in Pages on Mac
    5. Check and update your revised user documentation for accessibility, using the following links if you need instructions:
      • Improve accessibility with the Accessibility Checker (for Office and for Microsoft 365)
      • Accessibility for Google Docs, Sheets, Slides, & Drawings
      • Create accessible documents, spreadsheets, or presentations with Pages, Numbers, or Keynote
    6. Save your document as a PDF to ensure that your document’s layout and design are not changed when you upload the file to Canvas.
    7. Open and check that your PDF shows the document design you intend before submitting the file.

    Reflection Memo

    1. Write your Reflection Memo in your word processor AFTER you have revised your user documentation.
    2. Explain the changes that you made to your User Documentation in the sections of your memo.
    3. Connect your revisions to the course readings:
      • Refer to specific parts of the readings to explain the revisions you made. For example, if you increased the contrast between headings and the body, connect this decision to advice you read about in a textbook or in a reference module.
      • Footnote your sources.
    4. Use these instructions for the seven required section of the memo:

      Section 1: Memo Headers

      Follow the details on the Correspondence: Memo Components page to add the document title and memo headers.

      Section 2: Introduction

      • Briefly introduce the document you have revised and redesigned: its title, its intended audience, etc.
      • Connect to the original, unrevised version of the user documentation using one of these methods:
        • Link to the original version (the unrevised version) of the user documentation online.
        • Upload a copy of the original version with your submission, and refer to the attached original in your Introduction.
      • End with a purpose statement that indicates
        • your overall evaluation of the original document’s usability.
        • a summary of the changes you have made to the document.
        • what effect you believe these changes have had on its rhetorical success and overall usability.

      Section 3: Plain Language Revision

      • Begin with an evaluation of the original document’s usability in terms of its writing style.
      • Discuss the document’s language, addressing these questions:
        • Is the language plain, overly formal and jargon-filled, or a mixture of both?
        • How easy is the original content to understand?
        • What rules of plain language does the content break?
        • How does your revision alleviate these problems and improve usability?
      • Give examples of original content and your revisions.

      Section 4: Redesign

      • Begin with an evaluation of the original document’s usability in terms of design.
      • Discuss the document’s original design. What rules of design does it break (contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity, white space, typography)? Provide examples. What changes have you made and how do your changes improve usability?
      • Give examples of the original design and your revisions.

      Section 5: Ethical, Legal, and Cultural Considerations

      • Begin with an evaluation of the original document’s ethical, legal, and cultural considerations.
      • Discuss the document’s consideration of these aspects: What ethical, legal, and cultural considerations does it exclude? Provide examples. What changes have you made and how do your changes improve usability?
      • Give examples of the original design and your revisions.

      Section 6: Rhetorical Analysis

      • Introduce and discuss how the changes you have made to the document have increased its rhetorical power—its ability to connect with its audience.

      Section 7: Conclusion

      • Reiterate your evaluation of the original document and how your revisions/redesign have improved it in terms of rhetoric and usability.
      • Provide contact info in a sentence.
    5. Check your memo’s format for the following features:
      • Includes memo headers: to, from, date, and subject.
      • Uses single-spacing within paragraphs and double spacing between paragraphs.
      • Incorporates headings throughout the body to structure the document. Look back at the first- and second-level headings in the Survey of Writing template for examples.
      • Includes page numbers.
