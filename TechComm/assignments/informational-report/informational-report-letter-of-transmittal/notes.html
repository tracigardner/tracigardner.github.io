Outline: Letter of Transmittal for an Informational Report

The letter of transmittal introduces your report to its reader. It provides context, explains purpose, and sets an objective tone before the report begins. Keep it brief—usually one page, with 3–4 short paragraphs.

Paragraph 1: Purpose and Context

  • State that you are submitting the report and give its title.
  • Briefly explain why the report was prepared (the assignment, request, or decision context).
  • Example: “I am pleased to submit my informational report, Electric Vehicle Charging Access in Small Communities, prepared to inform city planners about equitable infrastructure options.”

Paragraph 2: Report Overview

  • Summarize the report’s subject in 2–3 sentences.
  • Explain what the report covers—its main sections or focus areas.
  • Emphasize that the report’s goal is to inform, not persuade.
  • Example: “This report defines current charging technologies, presents recent usage data, and outlines accessibility considerations for non-urban drivers.”

Paragraph 3: Audience and Use

  • Identify the intended readers or decisionmakers.
  • Explain how the report may assist them in understanding or evaluating the topic.
  • Example: “The information is intended to support local officials and community advocates as they evaluate future transportation initiatives.”

Paragraph 4: Closing and Courtesy

  • Thank the reader for their attention or the opportunity to prepare the report.
  • Offer to provide additional information if needed.
  • End with a professional closing (e.g., “Sincerely”) and your name.
  • Example: “Thank you for the opportunity to explore this topic. Please let me know if I can provide any further clarification.”

 

  1. Your address / header (or use your program/university letterhead if permitted).
  2. Date: right below your address (no extra line space between them).
  3. Inside address (full name, title, organization, mailing address of the decision-maker(s)).
  4. Subject line (optional but recommended): short, informative.
  5. Salutation (e.g., “Dear Councilmember Alvarez and Members of the Town Council,”).
  6. Opening paragraph: purpose of the letter and what you’re transmitting.
  7. Body paragraph(s) that briefly:
    • State the problem/opportunity the report addresses.
    • Summarize methods/scope (very briefly).
    • Highlight 2–3 key findings/insights.
    • Present your main recommendation(s) or requested action.
    • Note any limitations or next steps, if relevant.
  8. Closing paragraph: express appreciation, offer availability for questions/presentation, and specify how to reach you.
  9. Complimentary close (e.g., “Sincerely,”).
  10. Signature block (typed name, title/role, department/program, university, email, phone).
  11. Enclosure line (e.g., “Enclosure: Informational Report on ___”).
