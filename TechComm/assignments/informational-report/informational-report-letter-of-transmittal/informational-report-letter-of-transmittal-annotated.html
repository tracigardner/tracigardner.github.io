Purpose: Your letter of transmittal introduces your report to its audience. It provides context, explains purpose, and sets an objective tone before the report begins. It’s the first thing your reader sees, so it’s important to make a good impression.

Length: Keep it brief—usually one page, with 3–4 short paragraphs.

I’m pleased to submit my informational report, “Understanding Copyright, Public Domain, and Creative Commons for Teen Artists.” This resource supports your role in mentoring the artists-in-residence who create art in the Teen Artists’ Studio. The report should provide you all the details you need to determine how to help these artists, writers, and digital makers protect their creative works and collaborate with other artists.

Paragraph 1: Introduction

  • Align with the left margin (no indentation).
  • Single space.
  • Skip a line before the next paragraph.

This report explains copyright, fair use, public domain, and Creative Commons (CC) licenses, collectively known as intellectual property rights. Information in the report comes from reliable, public sources including the U.S. Copyright Office, the international nonprofit organization Creative Commons, and the Artists Rights Society, which supports visual artists. While the discussion explores practical details on how intellectual property rights protect creative work in the U.S., it does not include in-depth legal information, deferring to the Board’s legal team for these details.

Paragraph 2: Project Overview

  • Align with the left margin (no indentation).
  • Single space.
  • Skip a line before the next paragraph.

These key findings are covered in the report:

  • Intellectual property rights include economic benefits such as selling and licensing an artist’s work and protections that provide attribution.
  • Copyright is automatic once a work is created, but enforcement can be costly.
  • The details of intellectual property rights can be confusing and can result in unintentionally restricting on how art circulates.

By understanding these intellectual property rights, the Board can determine how to support the artists who use the Studio.
