Writing a
Letter of Transmittal

 

What Is a Letter of Transmittal?

A 1–2 page letter that introduces a document that follows, such as a report or proposal, to its audience. Your letter of transmittal

  • Provides context.
  • Explains purpose.
  • Sets an appropriate tone.
 

 

Essential Details

Design for clarity. Write for people who are busy, curious, and not experts. Your fact sheet should answer these questions:

  • What is this about?
  • Why does it matter?
  • What should I know or do next?

What to Include

Title: Clear and specific.

Byline: Your name.

Introduction: Overview, significance, and outline of document.

Body: 3–5 sections with specific headings (“Body” is NOT a heading). Explain in bullets, stats, or short paragraphs.

Conclusion: Summary, quick facts, takeaways, and/or action steps.

Visuals:

  • At least 3 visuals, meeting these criteria
    • 1 data visualization required.
    • Photo allowed (1 maximum).
    • Placed near text reference.
  • Labels & references:
    • 2-part caption.
    • Explanation of visual.
    • Reference in the text.

Sources & Documentation:

  • 6–10 credible sources.
  • Footnotes, endnotes, or in-text references.
  • Full citations at the end, taking up less than an entire column or half of the second page.

Date: Creation or update date.

Letter of Transmittal Structure

Include these sections and information in this order:

  • Return Address: Use a real, complete address (or use your program/university letterhead if permitted)
  • Date: Immediately below the return address (no extra line space between them).
  • Inside address: List the full name, title, organization, mailing address of the decision-maker(s).
  • Subject line: Optional but recommended. Add a short, informative subject.
  • Salutation: Add your professional greeting (e.g., “Dear Councilmember Alvarez and Members of the Allenville Town Council,”).
  • Message Body: Include the following paragraphs:
    • Paragraph 1: Introduction: State the purpose of the letter and identify what you’re transmitting.
    • Paragraph 2: Project Overview: Explain the subject the report addresses, and summarize your methods and/or scope.
    • Paragraph 3: Key Findings: Highlight 2–3 key findings/insights.
    • Paragraph 4: Conclusion & Contact Info: Thank the reader, offer availability for questions/presentation, and specify how to reach you.
  • Complimentary Close: Add the closing (e.g., “Sincerely,”).
  • Signature: Skip space for your signature. You can insert a graphic of your signature or use a cursive font.
  • Typed Name: List your name, your title/role, and your department/program, as appropriate.
  • Enclosure Notation: Add details on the attached report (e.g., “Enclosure: Informational Report on ___”).
 

 

Quick Design Tips

  • Stick to one page (front and back).
  • Use white/blank space to separate sections.
  • Choose high-contrast colors (but avoid anything that clashes).
  • Use sans-serif fonts for readability.
  • Balance text and visuals.
  • Use a logical reading flow (Z-pattern or F-pattern).

Your Audience

Address the letter to the actual non-expert decision-makers in a local community you have chosen to receive your report (e.g., a town council, a nonprofit board, a neighborhood association).

Use real names and addresses for your audience. Your message should be prepared as if you were going to mail it out.

Assignment Requirements

Basic Requirements

  • Genre: Letter of Transmittal.
  • Length: 1 page maximum.

Formatting Requirements

  • Page size: 8.5 x 11 (portrait)
  • Format: Text running from left to right margin, and 1" margins all around.
  • Spacing:
    • Single or 1.15 spacing between lines.
    • Double space between paragraphs.
  • Paragraphs: Do not indent (Block letter format).
  • Font Choices: Arial, Calibri, Verdana, Aptos, or Helvetica
  • Help: See Letter of Transmittal Example with Formatting Details

Success Tips

  • Make a good impression. Your letter of transmittal is the first thing your readers will see.
  • Use plain language, avoid jargon, and expand your acronyms. Your reader won’t know the definitions and explanations you provide in your report.
  • Look up the addresses for your audience so that you have accurate details for your letter.
