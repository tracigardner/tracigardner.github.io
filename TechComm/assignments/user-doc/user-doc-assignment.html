User Document
Creating an Instructional or Support Resource

Your Task

Worth 10% of your course grade

This assignment must be connected to the topic of your Informational Report for Non-Expert Readers.

Prepare a user-facing document that explains, guides, or supports action related to the subject of your informational report. The document should demonstrate clear, usable communication tailored to a specific audience of non-expert readers and a specific situation.

Your user document may take one of several forms, depending on your topic and Audience:

  • Step-by-step instructions for using or interacting with the subject
  • A decision guide or flowchart to help users determine when and how to take action
  • A learning aid that explains key terms, concepts, or steps to newcomers
  • A troubleshooting guide to help users address common problems
  • A quick-start reference or how-to guide summarizing key actions
  • Another type of support document that fits the subject and audience

Choose your type of user doc after you've reviewed the Topic Selection Guidelines below.

Review Document Samples and the Deliverable Checklist as you work.

Assignment Learning Objectives

Hide this section Show this section

By completing this assignment, you will develop your ability to:

  • Translate specialized or technical information from your informational report into a clear, helpful document that supports real-world action or decision-making by a non-expert audience.
  • Design a usable, accessible document that meets the needs of a specific audience by applying plain language principles, strong visual communication strategies, and effective document design (CRAP: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity).
  • Choose the most appropriate user-facing document type (e.g., instructions, flowchart, learning aid, troubleshooting guide) based on your topic, the needs of your audience, and the social value or community issue connected to your report subject.
  • Demonstrate audience awareness by tailoring your tone, structure, and content to support users in understanding, navigating, or acting on a complex subject with ease and confidence.
  • Incorporate and explain visuals (such as diagrams, screenshots, or icons) to increase the clarity, accessibility, and effectiveness of your user document.
  • Reflect on your design and communication decisions by writing a clear, well-structured memo that explains how your document supports users and connects to your informational report topic.

    Assignment Requirements

    • Length (according to option):
      • Option 1: 2–3 pages for the user doc itself + 1 page for a brief cover memo (see below).
      • Options 2-6: 1 page or two, back and front, according to requirements of option + 1 page for a brief cover memo (see below).
    • Format:
      • Use headings and subheadings throughout
      • Use at least 4 useful and necessary visuals (diagrams, tables, icons, or screenshots; no clip art unless it serves a purpose)
      • Use numbered steps, bullets, callout boxes, or other design features as needed
    • Design: Follow principles of effective document design (CRAP: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity)
    • Tone: Helpful, clear, and informative — avoid academic or formal language
    • Citations: If you use any information from outside sources (definitions, procedures, diagrams), cite them using your chosen documentation style
    • Cover Memo: Submit a 1-paragraph memo addressed to your instructor that uses the three-step neutral news message:
      • Para 1: Explains the purpose of the memo (introducing your user document)
      • Paras 2 or 2+: Explains (1) who the intended audience is, (2) what the document is designed to help them do, and (3) how the document connects to your report topic. (If your paragraph gets too long, chunk at an appropriate spot!)
      • Last Paragraph: Close cordially and provide contact information

    Usability and Accessibility Requirements

    Your user document should be designed for clarity and ease of use. It must:

    • Follow plain language principles (short sentences, active voice, concrete words)
    • Use headings, numbered/bulleted lists, and white space for navigation
    • Define any unfamiliar terms for your audience
    • Include visuals to support understanding, such as diagrams, screenshots, flowcharts, or illustrations
    • Be well-organized and scannable: readers should be able to find the information they need quickly

    Success Tips

    • Introduce and explain the quotations that you use.
    • Include a heading for every section, except the Introduction. The heading for the Request for Approval section is optional.
    • Use specific details on your Gantt Chart. For example, rather than “Task 2”, state exactly what the Task is.
    • Use visuals wisely. Include only relevant figures, charts, or tables, and label them clearly.
