Before creating your one-page fact sheet, take time to think about the people you are writing it for. Your goal is to help everyday community members — those directly affected by the issue — better understand what’s at stake, how it may impact them, and what they can do (if anything) in response.

Your audience may include families, residents, workers, students, caregivers, or others in the community. These are the people served by the decision-makers and public systems to whom you addressed your informational report — but not necessarily involved in those systems themselves.

In this assignment, you will write a 500–700 word audience analysis that explores who your readers are and how you will meet their needs.

  • Write one paragraph for each of the six questions below.
  • Use a clear, informative heading for each paragraph.
  • Focus on plain language and real-world understanding.

Tip: Draft your analysis in Google Docs or Word, then copy and paste it into the assignment text box.

Assignment Objectives

By completing this assignment, you will be able to:

  • Identify a real-world community audience for a public-facing document.
  • Understand how audience needs and knowledge shape tone, content, and design.
  • Anticipate what community members may already know—and what they still need to understand—about an important issue.
  • Plan effective ways to present information clearly, concisely, and accessibly.
  • Consider how to build trust with readers and motivate their interest or action.
  • Practice using audience analysis to create user-friendly, purpose-driven documents.

========================================== Understanding your audience is a key skill in technical writing. Before you begin writing your informational report, you need to understand the audience you’re writing for. A well-informed audience analysis will help you shape a report that not only informs, but also persuades—giving your audience the tools they need to act on an issue that matters.

This activity will help you identify what your audience already knows, what they need to learn, and how best to present your information so that it is clear, useful, and persuasive.
