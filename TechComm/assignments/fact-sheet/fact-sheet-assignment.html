Informational Fact Sheet

Your Task

Worth 10% of your course grade

For your informational report for non-expert readers, you focused on a subject of social relevance related to your major—something that addressed an issue of social justice or equity for a particular community. You wrote to local decision-makers (such as a town council, nonprofit board, or school leadership), offering background, data, and recommendations to help them make informed choices and take action.
==========================

Convert the content from your Informational Report for Non-Expert Readers to a two-page informational fact sheet for a new audience of non-experts: constituents or community members served by the decision-makers you addressed in your report.

Your goal is to inform or educate your audience in a way that is

  • Visually engaging.
  • Easy to understand (using plain language).
  • Accessible to a general audience.
  • Focused on key facts, practical relevance, and clear visuals.

Your fact sheet should help everyday people understand what’s at stake, how the issue may affect them, and what they can do (if anything) in response.

This fact sheet will not only test your ability to adapt content for a new audience—it will help you practice the kind of accessible, equity-centered communication that makes a real difference in the world.

===============================================
Prepare a user-facing document that explains, guides, or supports action related to the subject of your informational report. Your assignment must be connected to the topic of your Informational Report for Non-Expert Readers.

Your User Document should demonstrate clear, usable communication tailored to a specific audience of non-expert readers and a specific situation. You’ll need to define the goal of your document clearly and ensure its structure, tone, and design are suitable for a real-world non-expert audience.

Your user document may take one of several forms, depending on your topic and audience. Choose your type of user document after you’ve reviewed the Topic Selection Guidelines below.

Review Document Samples and the Deliverable Checklist as you work.

Your Audience

Think of the report as a detailed briefing for leaders who need to decide something, while the fact sheet is a tool for sharing clear, usable information with the people those leaders serve.

Write your project for non-expert users—such as new users, community members, caregivers, students, or volunteers.

They should be able to use the document to better understand, use, decide about, or troubleshoot the subject you’ve been researching.

Assignment Requirements

--> Tip: Feel free to use a Canva or Word template to set up your fact sheet.

  • Length: 2 pages. No more than 2 pages. You must ensure that your content (text + images + resources list) completely takes up 2 pages but does not go over. Your Works Cited list or endnotes should not take up an entire column or half of the second page. Do not put your Works Cited or References section on a third page. Make sure you do not include a blank third page.
  • Required Text Components: title; introduction, internal body sections, and conclusion. Body sections should have titles. No titles needed for the intro and conclusion.
  • Required Visual Components: 3 required graphics (tables, charts, graphs) to summarize data; 1 photo is acceptable, with credit but must be in addition to the 2 charts or graphs; colors are acceptable if they match the ethos of the subject ("fit" with the subject; do not go overboard -- choose 1-2 colors overall). No clip art.
  • Design: Portrait orientation (8.5 X 11) required. See sample fact sheets for suggested guidelines about layout.
  • Sources: Six minimum but as many as needed to fully document your research; use MLA documentation or your preferred style; in-text citations + Works Cited or in-text numerical notations and endnotes. Fact sheets that do not have correct internal citations PLUS a Works Cited list or endnotes will receive a grade no higher than a 75.

Basic Requirements

  • Length (according to option):
    • Option 1: 2–3 pages for the user doc itself + 1 page for a brief cover memo (see below).
    • Options 2–6: 1 page or two, back and front, according to requirements of option + 1 page for a brief cover memo (see below).
  • Documentation: If you use any information from outside sources (definitions, procedures, diagrams), cite them in-text and at the end of the document using your chosen documentation style.

  • Plain Language: Use short sentences and other plain language techniques (like active voice and concrete words). Avoid jargon.
  • Tone: Helpful, clear, and informative—avoid academic or formal language. Write as if you are standing beside the user, guiding them personally.
  • Document Design: Follow principles of effective document design (CRAP: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity).

Fact Sheet Requirements

Focus on usability and accessibility. Your user document should be designed for clarity and ease of use. It must:

  • Follow the document structure guidance from 11b. Components of the User Document of the course textbook, modifying the information based on the option you’ve chosen.
  • Use headings/subheadings, numbered/bulleted lists, and white space for navigation.
  • Define any unfamiliar terms for your audience.
  • Include visuals to support understanding, such as diagrams, screenshots, flowcharts, or illustrations:
    • Use at least 4 useful and necessary visuals (diagrams, tables, icons, or screenshots; no clip art unless it serves a purpose).
    • Give each visual a two-part caption (Figure 1: Description of Figure 1).
    • Refer directly to the visual in the text before you show it (See Figure 1, as Figure 1 notes, etc.).
  • Be well-organized and scannable: readers should be able to find the information they need quickly.

Formatting Requirements

  • Page size: 8.5 x 11 (portrait)
  • Spacing: Single or 1.15 spacing between lines; double spacing between paragraphs (1 blank line)
  • Paragraphs: Do not indent
  • Font Choices: Arial, Calibri, Verdana, Aptos, or Helvetica—but just one! Don’t mix fonts.
  • Font Sizes: Be consistent. Headings should be larger than the body text. Each heading level should be larger than the next lower heading (e.g., Heading 1 is larger than Heading 2; Heading 2 is larger than Heading 3, and so on).

    TIP: The default heading sizes in Word and Google Docs follow this guideline automatically.

Success Tips

  • Choose the most relevant and actionable information from your report.
  • Use plain language—avoid jargon, acronyms, or overly technical explanations.
  • Use visual techniques like headings, bullet points, icons, and charts to organize your content clearly.
  • Include visuals such as maps, simple graphs, timelines, or illustrations if they clarify your points.
  • Focus on usability: your audience should walk away knowing what the issue is, why it matters to them, and what they can do.
