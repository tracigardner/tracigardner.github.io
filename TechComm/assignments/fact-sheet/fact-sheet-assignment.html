Fact Sheet

Your Task

Worth 10% of your course grade

Convert content from your Informational Report for Non-Expert Readers to a one-page (front and back) fact sheet that informs or educates an audience of non-expert readers about your topic in a way that is

  • Visually engaging.
  • Easy to understand (using plain language).
  • Accessible to a general audience.
  • Focused on key facts, practical relevance, and clear visuals.

Your fact sheet should help everyday people understand what’s at stake, how the issue may affect them, and what they can do (if anything) in response.

Review Document Samples and the Deliverable Checklist as you work.

Your Audience

Design your fact sheet for a new audience of non-experts: the constituents or community members served by the decision-makers you addressed in your report.

Understand the difference:

  • Audience for your informational report: non-expert leaders who need to decide something.
    Example: Town of Blacksburg Mayor & Town Council
  • Audience for your fact sheet: non-expert, everyday people who the leaders serve and who want to know more about the topic.
    Example: People who live and work in the Town of Blacksburg

Assignment Requirements

Basic Requirements

  • Length: 1 page (front and back). No longer and no shorter. Take up the full front and back. Make sure you do not include a blank third page.
  • Software Options & Required File Format: Whatever software you are comfortable with (e.g., Canva, Word, Firefly), as long as you can export your fact sheet as a PDF (required file format).
  • Sources & Documentation:
    • Six sources minimum but as many as needed to fully document your research.
    • Use the documentation system preferred by your field (e.g., CS majors use IEEE).
    • In-text citations + Works Cited or in-text numerical notations and endnotes.
    • Include your works cited list, references, endnotes, or bibliography at the bottom of the back page. Your list should not take up an entire column or half of the second page.
  • Plain Language: Use short sentences and other plain language techniques (like active voice and concrete words). Avoid jargon.
  • Tone: Helpful, clear, and informative—avoid academic or formal language. Write as if you are standing beside the user, guiding them personally.
  • Document Design: Follow principles of effective document design (CRAP: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity).

Fact Sheet Requirements

Focus on usability and accessibility. Your user document should be designed for clarity and ease of use. It must:

  • Required Text Components: title; introduction, internal body sections, and conclusion. Body sections should have titles. No titles needed for the intro and conclusion.
  • xxx
  • Use headings/subheadings, numbered/bulleted lists, and white space for navigation.
  • Define any unfamiliar terms for your audience.
  • Include visuals to support understanding, such as diagrams, screenshots, flowcharts, or illustrations:
    • Use at least 4 useful and necessary visuals (diagrams, tables, icons, or screenshots; no clip art unless it serves a purpose).
    • Give each visual a two-part caption (Figure 1: Description of Figure 1).
    • Refer directly to the visual in the text before you show it (See Figure 1, as Figure 1 notes, etc.).
  • Required Visual Components: 3 required graphics (tables, charts, graphs) to summarize data; 1 photo is acceptable, with credit but must be in addition to the 2 charts or graphs; colors are acceptable if they match the ethos of the subject ("fit" with the subject; do not go overboard -- choose 1-2 colors overall). No clip art.
  • Be well-organized and scannable: readers should be able to find the information they need quickly.

Formatting Requirements

  • Page size: 8.5 x 11 (portrait)
  • Document Layout: Portrait orientation (8.5 X 11) required. Consistent margin on all sides (Minimum of .5"; maximum of 1").
  • Spacing: Single or 1.15 spacing between lines; double spacing between paragraphs (1 blank line)
  • Paragraphs: Do not indent
  • Font Choices: Arial, Calibri, Verdana, Aptos, or Helvetica—but just one! Don’t mix fonts.
  • Font Sizes: Be consistent. Headings should be larger than the body text. Each heading level should be larger than the next lower heading (e.g., Heading 1 is larger than Heading 2; Heading 2 is larger than Heading 3, and so on).

    TIP: The default heading sizes in Word and Google Docs follow this guideline automatically.

Success Tips

  • Choose the most relevant and actionable information from your report.
  • Use plain language—avoid jargon, acronyms, or overly technical explanations.
  • Use visual techniques like headings, bullet points, icons, and charts to organize your content clearly.
  • Include visuals such as maps, simple graphs, timelines, or illustrations if they clarify your points.
  • Focus on usability: your audience should walk away knowing what the issue is, why it matters to them, and what they can do.
