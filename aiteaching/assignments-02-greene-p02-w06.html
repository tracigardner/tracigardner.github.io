Exploring Our “Reading Space”

Assignment created by Justin Greene (Department of English), with Copilot

This activity encourages students to apply rhetorical concepts to a familiar environment, deepening their understanding of how spaces can be “read” and analyzed. The photographic element adds a visual component that can be used for multimodal compositions.

Outcomes

Rhetorical Knowledge, Writing Processes, Multimodal Transformation, and Reflective Practice

Objective

Students will analyze and describe a personal reading space through the lens of rhetorical concepts: logos, pathos, ethos, kairos, and exigence.

Activity Steps

Space Selection

  • Choose a space where you frequently “read” or study.
  • This could be a personal space (bedroom, living room, etc.) or a public area (library, café, etc.).

Observation and Documentation

  1. Visit your chosen space at least twice throughout the week. Take notes on the physical characteristics, ambiance, and your personal feelings about the space.
  2. As you record notes, consider the rhetorical appeals the space makes:
    • Logos: How does the space’s layout or organization appeal to logic or practicality?
    • Pathos: What emotions does the space evoke? How do colors, textures, or objects contribute to this? How do people or lack of people affect the emotional quality of the space?
    • Ethos: How does the space reflect or establish credibility (e.g., presence of books, certificates)?
    • Kairos: How is the space suited (or not) for the current moment or purpose of reading? Does the space change over time—is it different at various points of the day?
    • Exigence: What need or demand does this reading space fulfill?
  3. Take 5–7 photographs that capture different aspects of the space.
  4. Try to capture at least one of the following shot types:
    • Establishing or wide angle shot
    • High angle shot
    • Medium shot
    • Close up

Weekly Submission:

  • Detailed description of “reading” space.
  • Notes on space and its rhetorical appeals.
  • 5–7 draft photographs.
