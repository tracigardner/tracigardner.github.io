Rationale

This capstone assignment empowers students to become AI mentors within their disciplines. By designing workshops, students deeply engage with AI’s potential in their fields, preparing them to lead responsibly in AI-integrated workplaces.

Objectives

  1. Master prompt engineering in a discipline-specific context.
  2. Evaluate AI’s impact on professional practices.
  3. Design learning experiences to guide peers.

Assignment

  1. Identify 3–5 ways AI could assist in your discipline (e.g., code generation for CS, experiment design for Biology).
  2. For each task, craft and refine prompts to generate high-quality outputs.
  3. Critically assess each output’s accuracy, originality, and usefulness.
  4. Design a 30-minute workshop for peers:
    • 10 mins: Introduce AI’s role in your field
    • 15 mins: Guide participants through task-specific prompts
    • 5 mins: Discuss responsible AI use in your profession
  5. Submit:
    • Workshop plan and materials
    • Prompt library with explanations
    • 500-word essay on AI’s promises and perils in your field

Expanded Task

Now, you’ll become an AI pioneer in your field. Identify cutting-edge ways AI can transform your discipline, then master prompt engineering to make these a reality. You’ll rigorously test AI’s outputs, ultimately designing a workshop that empowers your peers to ethically harness AI’s potential.

Key Steps to Success

  1. Discipline Foresight (Day 1–3)
    • Interview 2–3 professors or industry pros in your field.
    • Ask: “How might AI reshape our discipline in 5 years?”
    • Study 2–3 recent papers on AI in your field.
    • List 5–7 transformative AI applications, rank by impact.
  2. Application Deep Dives (Day 4–6)
    • Pick your top 3–5 applications.
    • For each, use AI to suggest implementation steps.
    • Example (Astronomy): “Detail steps to use AI for exoplanet atmosphere analysis.”
    • Refine each prompt 3–5 times, aiming for innovation.
    • Consult field experts to validate AI’s suggestions.
  3. Prompt Engineering Mastery (Day 7–9)
    • For each application, craft 3 prompt variants:
      • Basic: State the task simply.
      • Advanced: Add field-specific constraints and contexts.
      • Expert: Layer in latest research, edge cases, and ethical concerns.
    • A/B test each variant, noting differences.
    • Create a field-specific “Prompt Engineering Guide.”
  4. Rigorous Output Evaluation (Day 10–11)
    • For each AI-generated solution:
      • Fact-check against recent journals.
      • Run it by a subject expert.
      • Apply it to a real-world problem.
    • Create an “AI Output Evaluation Rubric” with criteria like accuracy, innovation, and ethical alignment.
    • Grade each output, reflect on what makes responses excel or fall short.
  5. Workshop Design (Day 12–13)
    • Structure your 30-minute workshop:
      • “AI Horizon” (5 min): Your field’s AI-driven future.
      • “Prompt Mastery” (15 min): Guide peers through crafting expert prompts in real-time collaborative exercises.
      • “Critique Corner” (5 min): Apply your rubric to assess outputs.
      • “Ethics Epilogue” (5 min): Discuss AI’s ethical dimensions in your profession.
    • Design an interactive handout or digital worksheet.
    • Plan a mini “AI Ethics Oath” drafting activity.
  6. Workshop Delivery & Reflection (Day 14)
    • Run your workshop for peers or a student club.
    • Gather feedback via a short survey.
    • Write your 500-word “AI’s Promise & Perils” essay, incorporating:
      • Workshop insights
      • Personal views on AI’s field impact
      • Call to action for responsible innovation

Progression of Learning

These assignments progressively build students’ understanding and skills:

  1. Personal exploration fosters curiosity and critical thinking.
  2. Collaborative creation teaches prompt engineering basics.
  3. Case studies deepen ethical reasoning through teamwork.
  4. Discipline workshops empower students as AI leaders.

Throughout, students learn that AI is a powerful tool that amplifies human creativity and problem-solving but requires careful guidance, critical evaluation, and ethical consideration. They move from passive users to active directors, preparing to use AI responsibly in their academic and professional lives.

    “I asked DALL-E to ‘create an image representing machine learning bias in facial recognition.’ The result showed a diverse group of faces partially obscured by binary code, with some faces more recognized than others. This visually captured the bias issue well. However, when I asked ChatGPT to explain quantum computing, it provided a generally accurate overview but oversimplified qubit states. This showed me AI’s strength in visual metaphors but its limitation in deeply technical domains.”
  2. Co-Creating (Policy Studies Student)
    Initial Prompt: “Suggest policies to address urban food deserts.”
    Refined Prompt: “Propose data-driven, community-led policies to transform urban food deserts in low-income areas of Chicago, considering transportation, local entrepreneurship, and cultural food preferences.”
    “The AI’s initial ideas were generic. As I refined my prompt with specifics, its suggestions became more targeted, like using city transit data to optimize new bus routes to supermarkets. This fed into my original idea of partnering with local entrepreneurs to open culturally relevant food markets.”

    Group’s Guidelines: “1) Contests must define AI’s role. 2) AI models should credit training artists. 3) Human must demonstrate creative direction.”
